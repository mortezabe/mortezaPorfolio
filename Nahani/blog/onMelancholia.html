On Melancholia

Lars von Trier’s Melancholia deconstructs the concept of melancholy not only through the plot and cinematic techniques, but also through the use of Wagner’s prelude to Tristan and Isolde. The movie’s intertextual reference to a composition that is the epitome of the Romantic notion of infinite longing (sehensucht) expresses a hidden layer of melancholy that is normally concealed from common understanding of the term.

Melancholy, sometimes taken as an equivalent term to depression, is commonly defined as being inactive and lacking desire—that is not feeling the demand or enthusiasm for reaching out to the world and using its resources for the sake of one's own pleasure. While the main character (Justine) of the movie is melancholic—as she appears to be someone who is not enjoying the sources of joy that are around her—her encounter with the world demonstrates a deeper understanding of her expectations from the world. To her, the human world around her, that is the socially and culturally conditioned space around her, is completely undesirable and her only response to such a world is her experience of melancholy, that is withdrawing from any desire or wish for the satisfaction of any such desire. And Justine’s apparent resignation from desiring is embodied in the form of a planet called the Melancholia.

But this is all what we see on the surface: Von Trier’s deconstruction of melancholy through intertextual references to the 19th-century musical romanticism opens a space for a novel understanding of the mood. The movie depicts melancholy not as the negation of desire but as the result of an infinite, intense desire that cannot be satisfied in the world. Furthermore, melancholy as portrayed in the movie goes beyond a merely psychological mood or state; it becomes more of an existential condition that offers an epistemology that seems to be a more authentic understanding of the world.

The movie is about a young woman who seems to be suffering from melancholy, living underwhelmed by some pensive dominant mood that has affected her life so strongly that she cannot feel happy in her own wedding. Right from the beginning of the movie, we learn that she does not like to eat or socialize. Each time she tries to spend time with other people (except with her nephew) she faces failure, as she does not receive the response she expects, which seems to be a mutual understanding of the condition. Alongside her dissatisfaction with the way others encounter the world’s tragic nature, there is another important theme: a planet called Melancholia is coming towards the Earth, which, based on the prediction of scientists, is going to pass by the Earth. While studies that show the planet is not going to hit the earth, there are some who think that it will hit the Earth bring about the end of the world.

The effect of this situation on Justine is ironic: when gradually this belief of the danger that is threatening the Earth becomes stronger and stronger, Justine begins to feel both livelier and more restful. There is an obvious fast improvement in her mood. Although this does not mean that she does not feel the melancholy anymore or feels happy and excited, the melancholy that was torturing her from inside is replaced by an objective perception of the word and its condition. In a sense, her inner melancholy has received an outer response from the world, and this is how Von Trier’s definition of Melancholia goes beyond a particular psychological mood and becomes an epistemological position about the world. The objectivity of Melancholia, a fact that cannot be denied but can be only hoped not to occur, becomes the right perception of the world and its possible motion towards the end. Hence, the melancholy she feels is the emotional expression of the instinctual awareness she has of the fact that the Earth is being hit by an object called Melancholia. The threatening planet is, therefore, the embodiment of the mood or the feeling that Justine is experiencing. And this is introduced as a fundamental truth. This provides a condition where the mood or psychological state in which she is living completely fits the reality outside.

On the surface we think that Justine is a person without any desire, a bored person who cannot engage with the world, other people, and even objects around her. She has lost her appetite or ability or any capacity to enjoy and appreciate the tastes and beauties of the world. But we gradually come to understand that on the contrary she has such a powerful desire some kind of desire or some type of desire that cannot be satisfied with the ways in which the world has been defined and organized.
Although Justine is not happy and she shouldn't be because she is melancholic and has a sad mood

Although Justine is not happy and she shouldn't be because she is melancholic and has a sad mood, there is a new layer in her experience of Melancholia. She finds her psychological mood the exact reflection of what's going on outside. What's going on outside in the external world corresponds to the inner feelings she’s experiencing. so her feeling her emotion becomes the truth or the true expression of the reality outside. Or, the other way around: the external reality becomes the real embodiment of her emotions. This becomes an important aspect of melancholy Justine is experiencing as scientists (here represented by John, who is Justine’s brother-in-law) could have an explanation and therefore a causal knowledge of the world outside but it doesn't mean that they can feel the truth of the world’s situation. This is why John the scientist commits suicide because he found his own existence only meaningful as long as the world existed: there needs to be a world for science to do its explanatory work; science is not interested in nothingness! John was able to offer an explanation for the world only before the world is doomed to be finished. There is no scientific meaning outside the world. On an opposing side, this is the beginning of Justine's world.

Justine, who was melancholic on two planes in the first half of the movie, is in a paradoxical—and ironic—way, a happy, melancholic person in the second half. The first half of the movie, which portrays Justine’s response to the social world around her represents not only her melancholic mood but also her anxiety as she is contextualized in an unauthentic communal life that does make any sense at all to her. The second half, however, depicts a genuine world in which her inner melancholy reflects the outer Melancholia. Her moods become the true reflection of the reality outside and she feels secure, restful, relaxed, and grounded in her psychological state. The whole meaning and intertextual references to the prelude to Wagner's Tristan and Isolde which dominates the sonic space of the movie from the beginning is its interrelation with von Trier’s deconstructed conception of melancholy and depression. Wagner’s famous composition is about desire. It is about a type of desire that is never fulfilled; a love or some kind of passion that does not receive any response in the world and the only satisfaction to it is death. [summary of Tristan and Isolde and concept of sehensucht] In Melancholia, the planet Melancholia is associated with desire. On the surface we think that Justine is a person without any desire, a bored person who cannot engage with the world, other people, and even objects around her. She has lost her appetite or ability or any capacity to enjoy and appreciate the tastes and beauties of the world. But we gradually come to understand that on the contrary she has such a powerful desire some kind of desire or some type of desire that cannot be satisfied with the ways in which the world has been defined and organized. The prelude is constructed of lingering dissonances and suspensions that never get resolved. It expresses the type of desire that cannot be satisfied. Wagner’s music Drama is about the nature of this desire; it is the sonic embodiment of unsatisfiable desire and this is why his music is perceived as strongly dramatic.

The naked scene in Melancholia is definitely one of the strongest manifestation of this deconstruction of melancholy as a mood of being in the world. While because of the Melancholia’s blue light the entire scene is blue, the sinuous yet graceful position of Justine’s naked body with her subtle moves is extremely erotic and awakening desire, what one might think melancholy has nothing to do. The music is also the sonic embodiment of this unending sehensucht for something that cannot be reached in this world.
