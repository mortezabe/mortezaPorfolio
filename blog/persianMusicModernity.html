In Hossein Alizadeh’s setting of Hafez’s most well-known masnavi, a tasnīf titled “āhuye vahshī” (“wild Gazelle”), there is a moment where the soft, and almost carefree coy of rāst-panjgāh mode suddenly shifts to an intense moment of the mode shūshtarī. This occurs when after the hopeful expression of a wish for the entrance of Khezr—who has a magic solution to any barren desert—the poet remembers those who did not survive this disastrous situation and asks us to recall their memories by “accompanying the raining clouds”, that is by crying. The instrumentalists respond to this moment by turning the emotional content of the opening motifs inside out when the same motifs return in the varied nostalgic mode of shūshtarī. [more description here!!!] مگر خضر مبارک پی درآید / ز یمن همتش کاری گشاید به یاد رفتگان و دوستداران / موافق گرد با ابر بهاران Alizadeh, more than anywhere else though, demonstrates the expressive power of the dastgāh he has chosen for his selected verses of Hāfez’s poem where the music returns to rāst-panjgāh. This musical moment highlights the complex feeling of the verse in which Hafez combines the sorrowful expression of separation by using the simile of “blade/sword” and quickly shifting into the sarcastic and bitterly humorous complaint of the unfair treatment of the friendship: “translation of ….” چنان بیرحم زد تیغ جدایی / که گویی خود نبوده‌ست آشنایی The first mesrā (hemistich) is set to the peak of shūshtarī’s expressive force completed by the opening phrase of the second mesrā, before the last syllable of “nabūdast” (“has not been”) returns the mood/mode to the playful melodic formula of rāst-panjgāh with its quick tahrīr (melisma) on a stepwise descending fourth to the khātemeh (final) on the final syllable of the word “āshnāī” (“acquaintance”). This is the same word we heard at the end of the first verse with the same musical setting: in the first verse it was rhyming with the question “kojāī?” (“where are you?”) and now with “separation.” The same setting not only remembers but also confirms the acquaintance with the “wild gazelle,” the beautiful beloved who has run away. Here, we see that the problem is not probably the wild gazelle who has decided to run away but the nature that is not green any longer. One must cry to compensate for the lack of rain which has caused the separation. The final verse, being set to a triumphant musical closure, hints at some kind of hope. It is a strange kind of hope though: it invites us to perfume our soul with the “aroma of hope” eternally! The resonance of the word “eternally” with the high final note is transient, just as the fragrance of hope is probably transient. Seemingly paradoxical, it is only through the fragrance as well as sonic memory that one has to keep the hope, a subtle observation about the psychological conditions of Iranians in two completely separate yet perceived similar historical contexts seven centuries apart. The realist relationship between text and music is not limited to the above-mentioned moments. At the beginning of the tasnīf, the opening verse is very playful and the notes jump from here to there, not a common gesture in Persian music, which hints at the search for the āhuye vahshī. The coy and playfulness of rāst-panjgāh and the melodic and rhythmic language Alizadeh has chosen for this verse paints an image of a lover looking for his beloved as if in a hide-and-seek game. However, the game becomes gradually serious when we learn that the āhuye vahshī is probably the subject himself, a wanderer (just like āhuye vahshī) looking for people he has lost. Is the poetic voice speaking to itself?
