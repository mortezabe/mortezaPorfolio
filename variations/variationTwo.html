Musical Abstraction Deconstructed...

Morteza Abedinifard

Theme:

Variation:

Why does music matter? Music Matters for many reasons some of them are the reasons we share with one another (let’s call them more universal reasons) and some of them are personal reasons that make music specifically significant for each of us. However, there is one reason that I think can connect all of us to music. The reason I’m interested in can to some extent explain what is there in music that makes its experience an integral part of our lives. A general formulation of that reason is: music connects us to something beyond our specific individual beings, and I think that’s also the source of joy in music. In other words, there is something in music that connects the moment of listening to music to something beyond that moment, the individual listening to music to something beyond the individual; that could explain why in those moments when musical experience dominates our being in the world, everything else seems to be trivial, seems to be unimportant and insignificant.

Think of a time when you were listening to music and you felt you want to cry or you actually cried or you felt so much joy or so much sorrow or so much of some other particular type of feeling inside you that made you feel you were outside that particular circumstance, that is outside the time and place. We sometimes describe it as moments of ecstasy or moments of contemplation or immersing ourselves in an experience. That moment is related, among other things, to some degree of abstraction that is usually ascribed to music—in one way or another. Whether we think that music is a purely abstract art—which includes these mysterious sonic forms that move and play around without meaning anything causing pleasure in us only by giving us the chance to follow the motion of these sonic beings—or we believe that music is a representational art that speaks to us says something, the most important thing here is that by listening to music we can never agree to some pre-given ideas that are being expressed in music. The very fact that notwithstanding any interpretation we have of a piece, we always consider some degree of validity for other understandings and feel less biased than when we encounter a different interpretation of a poem, let’s say, shows that we never have any solid referential grounds for proving other interpretations wrong.

Music, in this sense, offers a unique and quite exceptional intellectual/emotional democracy which doesn’t even exist in abstract paintings. That democracy emerges from the freedom inherent in music, that is exactly what connects us to something beyond us as particular beings. If music had a particular meaning like the way language does (we all know that even sometimes languages doesn’t easily contain fixed and given meanings), then music would have never been able to make us feel disconnected from the particular moment we are living in. Music’s very power of ‘distraction’, reflected in the way we transcend or in a sense ‘unhear’ the world we that we are in, is the same force that turns the experience of listening to music a path for connecting to something beyond our particularity. It’s an invitation to being universal, and communal. In this manner, musical abstraction is equally political.
