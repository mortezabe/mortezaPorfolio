Concert Hall: Imprisonment of Music's Power

Theme:

Music has the power to distract you from everyday life so much so that you could put yourself in danger. This is best captured in Odysseus: “Odysseus and the sirens. Book 12 of Homer’s 13 Odyssey emphasizes the overwhelming power of music, both for good and for ill. The sirens—part woman, part bird—sing so beautifully that seafarers forget all worldly concerns and die when their ships strike the nearby rocks. To succumb to the beauty of music means to turn one’s back on life. “But if you wish to listen,” Circe tells Odysseus, “let the men tie you . . . hand and foot, back to the mast, lashed to the mast, so you may hear those harpies’ thrilling voices; shout as you will, begging to be untied, your crew must only twist more line around you and keep their stroke up, till the singers fade.” As his crew members continue to row with beeswax in their ears, Odysseus gains wisdom by hearing the sirens’ song, all the while straining against and begging to be freed from the ropes.” (Mark Evan Bonds)
David Alfaro Siqueiros, George Gershwin in a Concert Hall, 1936

Variation:

The concert hall, which is apparently a place to worship music in a temple-like condition, is indeed nothing but the imprisonment of music’s power. The concert hall controls music’s destructive forces and keeps them ‘away’ from the society. But where does this ‘life-threatening’ force of music come from? Ironically, it comes from its resistance against ways in which life is structured. Music cannot hide its meaninglessness and any effort to make it meaningful is at the same time admitting the impossibility of such an endeavour. Creating a special space ‘outside’ life to control the storm of this meaninglessness is the modern solution for regulating music’s destructive force.

From this perspective, the ‘semantic’ threats music makes against human life have been the main reasons music has been kept under control, whether in the church or court or in concert halls. Musical vagueness is dangerous to human being as it challenges the certainty assumed for leading everyday life. While music cannot be reduced to abstract sounds and it definitely has strong social and cultural functions, its place in life has always been unclear being perceived as an abstract art; it is exactly these abstract and otherworldly layers of music that could turn into forces that run against the sociality of life. Ironically, the politics of music destructs the political life.
